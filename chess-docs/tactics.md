# Tactics

## Defensive

### Perpetual Check
One player delivers an unending series of checks from which the opponent cannot escape, resulting in a draw by threefold repetition. Typically used by the weaker side to avoid a loss.

### Playing for Stalemate
A drawing resource where the losing side maneuvers so that they have no legal moves but are not in check. The technique involves giving away material to eliminate legal moves or engineering a position where the opponent cannot continue without causing stalemate.

## Advantages

### Weak Color Complex
A group of squares of one color that cannot be defended by pawns, because those pawns have moved to the opposite color. Since pawns cannot move backward, such weaknesses are permanent. The opponent exploits the complex by placing pieces (especially a bishop of that color) on those squares.

### Good Bishop vs. Bad Bishop
A bishop is "good" if it moves freely and is not blocked by its own pawns; it is "bad" (sometimes called a "tall pawn") if most of its own pawns stand on the same color squares, severely limiting its scope. The principle: place your pawns on the opposite color from your bishop.

### Knight vs. Bishop — Which Is Best When
Neither piece is unconditionally superior; it depends on the pawn structure. Bishops excel in open positions with long diagonals and pawns on both flanks. Knights excel in closed positions and when anchored on a stable outpost that no enemy pawn can challenge.

### Advantage in Development
One player has more pieces actively posted and ready to fight. This is a dynamic, time-sensitive advantage: if not converted through active play quickly, the opponent will finish developing and the advantage evaporates.

### Space Advantage
Controlling more squares on the board, typically through an advanced pawn chain, restricting the opponent's pieces to a cramped area. The player with more space can maneuver freely; the cramped player has difficulty coordinating.

### Having a Strong Center
Controlling the central squares (d4, d5, e4, e5) with pawns gives greater piece mobility, more attack routes, and the ability to launch flank attacks on a solid foundation.

### Control Over Open Files, Ranks, and Diagonals
Rooks on open files, rooks on the 7th rank, bishops on long open diagonals, and queens on open lines generate concrete threats and restrict the opponent's king and pieces.

## Mating Patterns

### Fool's Mate
The fastest possible checkmate (two moves): White weakens the king's diagonal catastrophically by moving both f- and g-pawns, allowing the queen to deliver checkmate immediately.

### Scholar's Mate
A four-move checkmate exploiting the weak f7 square. The queen and bishop team up to attack the undefended f7 pawn in front of the uncastled king.

### Back Rank Mate
A rook or queen delivers checkmate along the opponent's first rank when the king is trapped behind its own unmoved pawns with no escape squares.

### Smothered Mate (Philidor's Legacy)
A knight delivers checkmate to a king surrounded ("smothered") by its own pieces. The knight's jumping ability makes it the only piece that can reach the king through such a blockade. The typical Philidor's Legacy procedure: knight check → queen sacrifice to force a rook next to the king → knight delivers the smothering check.

### Opera Mate
A rook delivers checkmate on an open file, protected by a bishop on the diagonal, which simultaneously covers the king's escape squares.

### Anastasia's Mate
A knight and rook team up to trap a king against the side of the board. The rook delivers checkmate along the h-file (or a-file), the knight controls two key escape squares, and a friendly pawn or piece blocks remaining flights.

### Boden's Mate
Two bishops deliver checkmate on criss-crossing diagonals to a king whose flight squares are blocked by its own pieces.

### Legal's Mate
An apparent queen sacrifice followed by checkmate from two knights and a bishop. Black pins White's f3 knight to the queen; White ignores the "pin" and sacrifices the queen — if Black takes, the knights and bishop deliver checkmate.

### Arabian Mate
A knight and rook coordinate to trap the king in a corner: the rook delivers checkmate, the knight simultaneously defends the rook and covers the king's two remaining escape squares. One of the oldest known mating patterns, described in 8th century Arabic manuscripts.

### Epaulette Mate
The king is in check with two parallel escape squares on either side occupied by its own pieces (resembling military shoulder decorations), making the check also checkmate. Typically occurs in the center of the board.

### Dovetail Mate (Cozio's Mate)
A queen delivers checkmate where the king has two diagonal flight squares blocked by its own pieces, and the checking queen cannot be captured. The visual pattern resembles a dove's tail.

### Greco's Mate
A bishop and rook coordinate against a castled king: the bishop controls the long diagonal while the rook delivers checkmate along the h-file.

### Morphy's Mate
A bishop and rook coordinate to deliver mate: the bishop controls one escape route, and the rook mates along the back rank. The king is typically cornered with a friendly pawn blocking its only escape square.

### Pillsbury's Mate
A bishop controls a long diagonal while a rook delivers checkmate along an open file next to the castled king. Requires the opponent's pawn shield to be compromised.

### Hook Mate
A rook and knight deliver checkmate with a supporting pawn completing the pattern — visually resembling a hook. The pawn and knight restrict the king while the rook delivers the coup de grâce.

### Lawnmower Mate (Corridor Mate)
Two major pieces (two rooks, or a rook and queen) systematically drive the king to the edge of the board, "mowing" it rank by rank or file by file until it is cornered and mated.

### Damiano's Mate
A queen and pawn coordinate to mate a king: the queen delivers check while a pawn covers the king's one possible flight square.

### Lolli's Mate
A queen arrives on h6 supported by a well-advanced pawn, creating a mating threat that is virtually impossible to defend once established.

### Blackburne's Mate
Two bishops and a knight coordinate to deliver checkmate, with the pieces working in harmony on interlocking squares.

## Tactical Tools

### Pin
A piece is under attack but cannot (or should not) move because doing so would expose a more valuable piece behind it. An **absolute pin** is against the king (the pinned piece cannot legally move); a **relative pin** is against a queen or other valuable piece (the piece can legally move but at a material cost).

### Double Attack
One move creates two or more simultaneous threats, forcing the opponent to deal with one while the other succeeds.

### Skewer
The inverse of a pin: a more valuable piece is attacked, forced to move to safety, and in doing so exposes a less valuable piece behind it to capture.

### Deflection
Forcing an enemy piece away from a critical defensive duty — guarding a key square, defending another piece, or maintaining a pin — so that what it was protecting can be exploited.

### X-ray
A long-range piece exerts influence "through" an intervening piece. Can be offensive (attacking a target on the far side of a blocking piece) or defensive (two friendly pieces defending each other through an enemy piece).

### Trapping a Piece
Maneuvering an enemy piece into a position where it has no safe squares to escape capture, typically by cutting off its retreat routes with pawns or pieces.

### Clearance
Vacating a square, file, or diagonal — sometimes by sacrificing the piece occupying it — so that another piece can use that line or square more effectively.

### Unpinning
Breaking a pin that has been imposed on one of your pieces, either by capturing or chasing away the pinning piece, blocking the pin with another piece, or moving the piece the pin was aimed at.

### Capturing a Defender (Removing the Guard)
Eliminating a piece that is performing a key defensive role, thereby exposing the pieces or squares it was protecting. The defender can be removed by capturing it directly, attacking it so it must move, or overloading it with more duties than it can handle.

### Blocking
Interposing a piece between an attacking piece and its target, breaking a line of attack or cutting off a defender from where it is needed.

### Quiet Moves
Non-capturing, non-checking moves that create a decisive threat — typically completing a mating net or winning material — without any forcing element. Often the hardest moves to find because they appear passive but are lethal.

### Decoy
Luring an enemy piece to a specific square where it becomes vulnerable to a tactical blow or where it obstructs its own defenses. Unlike deflection (which drives a piece away), a decoy draws a piece toward a particular square.

### Intermediate Move (Zwischenzug)
From the German for "in-between move": instead of making the expected response (typically a recapture), a player first inserts a powerful intermediate threat that the opponent must address, only then completing the originally expected sequence. The zwischenzug often completely changes the evaluation of the position.

### Intermediate Check (Zwischenschach)
A specific type of zwischenzug where the interposed move is a check, gaining a tempo while forcing the opponent's king to move before the sequence resumes.

### Interference
Placing a piece between two connected enemy pieces to sever their coordination — for example, cutting a rook off from the king it is defending, or separating two pieces that jointly control an important square.

### Fork
A single piece attacks two or more enemy pieces simultaneously, making it impossible to save both.

### Discovered Attack
A piece moves, and in doing so uncovers an attack by a piece behind it on the same rank, file, or diagonal. The moving piece is free to make its own threat simultaneously, creating two threats at once.

### Discovered Check
A discovered attack where the revealed piece gives check to the enemy king. The moving piece can make any threat it likes, since the opponent must first deal with the check.

### Destroying the Pawn Shield
Sacrificing material to rip open the pawns in front of the castled king, exposing it to direct attack. The most classic version is the "Greek Gift" sacrifice (Bxh7+).

### Blocking the Escape Square
Preventing the enemy king from fleeing to a safe square, typically completing a mating net. Accomplished by placing a piece on a flight square, using a pin, or exploiting a friendly piece already standing on a key escape square.
